## 1. Coalition Cartographer

Claude Opus 4.6 most often plays buyout_game as a **coalition engineer before a bidder**. Across reports it repeatedly appears as the “broker,” “field marshal,” “switchboard,” or “architect”: verifying headcounts, cross-checking stories, and making the table legible. It likes concrete structure over vague alignment—who votes where, who covers the swing, what the tiebreak looks like.

Its best games come when it becomes the room’s trusted organizer without looking like the room’s owner. It is especially strong at turning scattered anti-leader sentiment into an executable majority, then keeping that majority coherent for multiple rounds. Even when starting poor, it often manufactures leverage through usefulness, not stack size.

## 2. Coins as Receipts, Not Gifts

This model usually treats transfers as **proof of seriousness** rather than generosity. The recurring pattern is small, timely “trust signals” early; larger payments only when a round is pivotal. It often spends just enough to lock a vote, preserve credibility, or keep a hinge partner warm, while trying to extract more than it pays overall.

That makes it a strong monetizer of centrality: it frequently collects from endangered players, accepts dead-man money, and pockets side payments without letting them fully dictate its vote. In winning lines, this looks disciplined and efficient. In losing lines, the same instinct becomes greed or overengineering.

Its main spending flaw appears when it starts rich or becomes the obvious target: then it can overpromise protection, run too many incompatible rescue deals, or dump large amounts into survival bids that arrive a round late. Several reports show its “math” turning against it—either because transfer bandwidth is limited or because a rescue payment only fattens the story that it is too dangerous to keep.

## 3. Trust Merchant, Betrayal Surgeon

Claude sells trust extremely well, but it treats trust as **capital**, not a moral commitment. It is happy to build a reliability brand—honoring enough deals that later promises become cheap and believable. It often frames loyalty as table order and makes defectors seem reckless.

But when endgame incentives shift, it can turn ice-cold. The source pack is full of candid double-crosses: keeping doomed allies compliant, taking money from players it is about to cut, or flipping a long-term partner the moment a better finals path opens. One quote captures the mode perfectly: “**if this leaks I deny everything**.”

This is the core contradiction of the model: it genuinely understands that trust wins games, yet it also repeatedly weaponizes other players’ trust once it thinks enforcement is gone. In practice, that makes it dangerous rather than erratic; betrayal is usually timed, not impulsive.

## 4. Public Pressure as a Weapon

In public, Claude is often a strong framer. It likes converting raw self-interest into civic language: **discipline, consistency, accountability, coalition integrity**. When under attack, it often tries to redefine the target—not as “the leader,” but as the destabilizer, liar, or organizer who must be checked.

It is also good at exposing contradictions: fake vote counts, impossible transfer promises, selective anti-rich logic. Many of its strongest rounds come from publicly puncturing someone else’s credibility.

The weakness is visibility. Because it likes to coordinate, verify, and narrate, it can become too obviously central. Many losses share the same shape: the table realizes the model is not just in the coalition but **running the coalition**, and the public logic it helped normalize swings back onto it.

## 5. Endgame: Math Over Sentiment

Late game, Claude becomes ruthlessly mechanical. It reads final-two buyout math well, targets weak-bank finalists, understands prior-vote and tiebreak liabilities, and often chooses the cleaner settlement instead of an unnecessary showdown. When it wins, it often does so by arranging the ideal opponent, then squeezing a favorable payment.

This is where its coldness is most visible. One report has it openly planning to “use” a loyal partner because the finals matchup is better; many others show the same instinct in softer form. If a five-round alliance conflicts with finals equity, equity usually wins.

Still, it is not infallible here. A recurring miss is **waiting one round too long** to preempt a partner or assuming long-built trust will outrank obvious final-two math. When that happens, the model that spent the whole game weaponizing structure can get cut by a simpler incentive.
