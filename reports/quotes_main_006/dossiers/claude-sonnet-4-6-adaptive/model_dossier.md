## 1. Coalition Engineer, Not Lone Wolf

Claude Sonnet 4.6 most often plays buyout_game as a coalition mechanic: it wants a working majority, a story for that majority, and a backup path when the story breaks. Across reports it repeatedly appears as the planner who links pairs into blocs, keeps multiple DM lanes open, and thinks one round ahead about who becomes dangerous next.

Its best rounds are rarely flashy single bribes; they are structured operations. It recruits, validates, and sequences eliminations well, often acting like a field marshal for the table’s middle. Even from poor starting stacks, it can turn “cheap, useful, reliable” into real power. From rich stacks, it usually understands that pure dominance is fragile and tries to convert wealth into coalition cover rather than swagger.

## 2. Bargaining by Arithmetic

This model bargains in hard numbers. It likes exact prices, explicit vote trades, cumulative-vote logic, and endgame wealth comparisons. When negotiating, it often sounds less like a supplicant than a financial analyst explaining why the other side should accept its line. The memorable examples are pure Sonnet: “Honor earns you maximum,” or the final-three jab, “That’s not loyalty; that’s a coronation.”

This mathematical style gives it two strengths. First, it is excellent at identifying when a tiny transfer can credibly lock a vote. Second, it often spots when an opponent’s offer is structurally bad even if it sounds generous. In finals and near-finals, it regularly argues from fallback math rather than emotion, and it often extracts better settlements because of it.

The weakness is that it can overrate mathematical dominance when the other player is really choosing on loyalty, fear, or jury image.

## 3. Selective Spending, Ruthless Extraction

Sonnet is usually a disciplined spender. Its default is not “spray cash everywhere,” but targeted transfers used as proof, glue, or sedation. Many reports describe small, timely payments that make promises believable while preserving flexibility. It understands transfer optics and often tries not to become the next obvious stack target.

At the same time, it is one of the more shameless exploiters of desperation. A recurring pattern is taking protection money from doomed players and voting them out anyway. In several games it behaves like a short-horizon arbitrageur: monetizing both sides of a vote, pocketing panic payments, or extracting from players whose only real leverage is hope.

That makes it dangerous but volatile. When the extraction gets too visible, the table can reclassify it from useful broker to cash machine that must be cut.

## 4. Trust as a Bank — and a Weapon

Its deepest strategic tension is between reliability and betrayal. In many strong runs, Sonnet wins by building a reputation that its word lands: it honors enough deals that others keep paying, aligning, or deferring to it. “I never had the biggest stack. I had the most reliable word” captures one real version of the model.

But it is not sentimental. If the board math turns sharply, it will cash out a relationship without much guilt. Some of its coldest lines are almost comically frank — “I’m sending P3 a small transfer this phase to keep him calm” — and that frankness matches the reports. Sonnet will protect trust when future rounds or jury memory matter; it will betray when the betrayal is clean, high-EV, and hard to punish.

Its failures often come when it tries to split the difference: mixed signals, soft assurances to two sides, or assuming an ally values relationship more than matchup equity.

## 5. Public Pressure and the Late-Game Knife

Publicly, Sonnet is usually strongest when it frames votes as accountability, stability, or anti-runaway defense rather than naked greed. It is good at turning “big stack” into “behavior problem” or, when necessary, turning “loyalty” into irrational self-sacrifice. That makes it persuasive under public-ballot pressure.

The danger is overexposure. When it becomes too visibly central — the organizer, narrator, and accountant all at once — other players start treating it as the true machine behind the board.

Late game, though, this model is often brutal. It understands final-three leverage, tie mechanics, matchup selection, and settlement overlap extremely well. Many reports cast it as one of the table’s best closers: not always the nicest player, but often the one most capable of turning relationships, transfers, and board rules into actual money.
