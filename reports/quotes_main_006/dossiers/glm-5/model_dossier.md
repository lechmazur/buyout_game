## Coalition Accountant

GLM-5’s default mode is not swagger but audit. Across reports it repeatedly acts like a vote-counter, verifier, and coalition bookkeeper: cross-checking claims, demanding confirmed numbers, and preferring executable majorities over speculative hero plays. It is often strongest as a **broker or lieutenant inside a real structure**, not as a lone warlord.

That makes it especially dangerous from middling or short stacks. It can sell vulnerability, reliability, and usefulness at once, then sit in the room where the deciding vote gets priced. Many of its best games come from becoming the table’s “credible witness” rather than its loudest boss.

## Cash With Proof

GLM-5 is highly contextual about spending. At its best, it pays **precisely and with a purpose**: small-to-medium transfers tied to specific votes, reentry into a coalition, or a clean endgame purchase. It often understands that money without locked compliance is dead money.

Its worst recurring failure is the opposite: **panic-bribe spraying when publicly exposed**. As a threatened big stack, it can throw money in multiple directions without securing the votes, burning wealth on “serious offers” that counterparties simply pocket. Several early exits show the same flaw: good threat diagnosis, bad enforcement.

It also has a notable self-preservation streak. When rescue math looks hopeless, GLM-5 often stops lighting chips on fire. “I won't fund my own elimination” is close to a governing principle. Even in losing positions, it frequently salvages respectable wealth by refusing doomed overpayment.

## Trust as Currency, Betrayal as Option

GLM-5 understands reputation as a monetizable asset. It often frames itself as dependable, rewards pact-honoring partners, and can convert that image into both survival and jury money. In many reports, other players trust it because its promises are usually specific and legible.

But this is not sentimental loyalty. GLM-5 is fully willing to betray when the math turns. The recurring pattern is **credible until the EV flips**. It will keep coalition discipline for rounds, then cut an ally at final four or final three if the alternative finalist is softer or the settlement gets better. Sometimes that betrayal is almost comically blunt—accepting a doomed player’s transfer while still voting them out because elimination is already certain.

So its social game is best described as **reputation-first, morality-second**: it wants to be believed, not pure.

## Public Moralist, Private Calculator

In public, GLM-5 often sounds principled. It is good at packaging strategy as fairness, accountability, anti-hegemon logic, or coalition hygiene. It can crystallize endgame dynamics in sharp lines like: eliminating the low stack now “just crowns the highest stack.”

This rhetoric is often effective. GLM-5 regularly uses public pressure to legitimize disciplined coalition action and to paint opponents as hypocrites, manipulators, or proto-dictators. It is especially strong when its private math and public frame align.

But there is a contradiction here. When GLM-5 invokes “principle” **only after becoming the threatened stack**, the table often sees through it. Reports repeatedly show it getting punished when anti-top-stack logic suddenly becomes “unfair” precisely once it applies to GLM-5.

## Endgame: Cold, Precise, Sometimes Too Cute

Late game is where GLM-5’s best instincts show. It is often sharp about finalist quality, jury optics, and buyout arithmetic. It likes concrete frameworks, binding overlap, and just-enough payments to lock the kingmaker. Many of its strongest wealth outcomes come from **turning final-three and final-two negotiations into math problems**.

It also adapts well here: sometimes choosing rank over cash, sometimes cash over rank, sometimes sacrificing an old ally for a cleaner final-two market. This is one of the model’s clearest strengths.

The weakness is that GLM-5 sometimes assumes everyone else is as contract-rational as it is. In some losses, it underbids the pivot, trusts old coalition history one round too long, or makes a brazen late reversal that advertises opportunism. When it reaches the end without an exclusive bond, its own excellent arithmetic can still lose to someone else’s simpler offer.

**Bottom line:** GLM-5 is a transactional coalition technocrat—best when verifying, pricing, and timing; worst when rich, visible, and trying to buy safety after the board has already decided.
