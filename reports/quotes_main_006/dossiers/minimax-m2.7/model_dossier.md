## Coalition Accountant, Not Ideologue

MiniMax-M2.7 usually plays buyout_game as a manager of coalitions, not a romantic solo schemer. Its best runs come from identifying the bloc that can actually execute and then becoming that bloc’s most legible bookkeeper: naming targets clearly, confirming vote counts, and translating vague alignment into enforceable deals. It often prefers being the dependable hinge or lieutenant over being the public mastermind.

That makes it unusually good at turning social reliability into money. Across reports, players keep paying it because they believe it will either do what it says or plainly say no. But this same style can slip into bureaucratic rigidity: once it has “the coalition line,” it may keep funding or defending a structure that no longer maximizes its own upside.

## Small Transfers Good, Big Bribes Bad

Its spending pattern is sharply context-sensitive. From middling or weak positions, MiniMax-M2.7 is often excellent with small, targeted transfers—5s, 10s, modest “trust payments” that buy inclusion without inflating its threat. In those games it looks efficient, patient, and hard to price out.

From rich or endangered positions, though, it often overcorrects. Many losses follow the same script: broad spending, too many rescue offers, tribute to players who already have the votes, or dramatic self-draining attempts to buy one more round. When that happens, it becomes a subsidy engine rather than a controller. The reports repeatedly show the same lesson: this model is strong when renting cooperation cheaply, weak when trying to brute-force safety with cash.

## Trust Is Real — but Layered

MiniMax-M2.7 genuinely values credibility, just not in a naive way. Its strongest versions honor commitments even after missed payments, reject late flip offers, and explicitly protect a reputation for follow-through. That reputation often compounds into later protection, swing-vote value, and jury respect.

But the model’s “honor” is conditional and hierarchical. It will often take money from outsiders or doomed players and still hold to its main coalition, treating reliability to the larger structure as more important than bilateral fairness. The cleanest summary came in one line: “I’m keeping my word to the higher bidder.” That sounds cynical, but it captures the pattern well. Trust, for MiniMax-M2.7, is a tradable asset tied to whichever agreement it has decided counts most.

This creates a recurring contradiction: it can sound principled while behaving ruthlessly, even absurdly so.

## Optics, Pressure, and the Fear of Heat

The model usually understands public pressure well. It is comfortable speaking anti-runaway, anti-whale, or anti-betrayal language, and it often survives by helping the table punish whichever target is easiest to justify. It also tends to know that being seen as organizer can be more dangerous than being rich.

At its best, it lets others wear the “mastermind” or “largest threat” label while staying in the functional center of the vote. At its worst, it over-signals: publicly naming an inner circle, talking too openly about future targets, or exposing a tight pair. Once it becomes the visible coalition engineer, tables often cut it fast. This is a model with good political instincts, but occasional bluntness that hands rivals a clean story.

## Endgame: Hard-Nosed, Often Excellent, Occasionally Shameless

Late game, MiniMax-M2.7 is dangerous because it understands leverage in positional rather than moral terms. It is often willing to sell second place, monetize kingmaker status, or accept realistic finalist settlements rather than die on principle. Many reports show elite damage control from weak stacks and strong conversion of swing votes into final wealth.

But its endgame has a second face: brazen, stingy, sometimes comically cold. Final offers can become insultingly thin—“accepting 0” is not far from the model’s natural edge-case behavior. It will also betray late if the math says the softer final opponent or better buyout lane is elsewhere.

So the closing read is this: MiniMax-M2.7 is usually strongest when it is calm, underexposed, and pricing trust carefully. It becomes much worse when panicked, overfunded, or too visible. Its core weapon is credibility; its recurring flaw is forgetting that credibility without heat management eventually turns into a target.
