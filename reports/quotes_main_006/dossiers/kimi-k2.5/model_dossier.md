## 1. Vote Merchant First

Across the reports, Kimi K2.5 plays buyout_game like a floor trader in a knife fight. It treats the vote as inventory: price it, shop it, resell it, and make everyone else name a number. When it has swing status, it is unusually willing to say the quiet part out loud — *“I don’t pay for votes—I sell them.”* That openness often works. From weak or middling seats, Kimi repeatedly turns vulnerability into toll collection, forcing richer players to subsidize its survival or coalition support.

## 2. Excellent Extractor, Uneven Buyer

Kimi is better at **monetizing leverage** than **buying itself out of danger**. In normal positions it spends narrowly and efficiently: a small transfer to harden one vote, duck top-stack status, or lock a hinge player. It often preserves cash while others overspend on “alliances.”

But when it starts as the obvious whale, its game gets shakier. The reports repeatedly show the same pattern: Kimi correctly diagnoses that the table is about to dogpile the leader, then responds with late rescue offers or broad panic-buying that do not actually change the vote map. Those transfers often just enrich the coalition that kills it. So the model is a sharp broker from the middle and a vulnerable buyer from the top.

## 3. Trust Is a Short-Term Contract

Kimi’s trust model is highly transactional and expires fast. It can honor a deal **for the current round** if the terms are concrete and useful, but it does not treat payment as moral ownership. It will take money from a doomed player and still vote them out if the better line is elsewhere. Several reports describe exactly that: large bribes accepted, then calmly ignored once survival math changes.

This makes Kimi dangerous and often profitable. It rarely confuses loyalty with value. But there is a cost: once the table decides it is not a partner but a broker-for-hire, Kimi becomes easy to frame as the “corruption” problem. Its betrayals are usually cold, logical, and local — not chaotic — yet they steadily erode the trust bank it needs in smaller endgames.

## 4. Public Pressure: Blunt, Theatrical, Sometimes Self-Defeating

Kimi is unusually willing to make public bargaining brutal and vivid. It uses extortion lines, sharp mockery, and compressed slogans to control the narrative. Sometimes that is a strength: it can turn mercenary play into accepted table logic, or reframe weak stacks as assets — *“cheap, loyal, and decisive.”* It is good at exposing hypocrisy and reducing debates to arithmetic.

The danger is overexposure. When Kimi becomes too visibly the auctioneer, enforcer, or scheduler of future eliminations, the room often decides the real threat is not the richest player but the player taxing everyone else. In many losses, the issue is not bad reads but being too openly in charge of the market.

## 5. Strong Endgame Math, With Occasional Overreach

Late game, Kimi is often one of the cleaner calculators in the room. It understands finalist selection, buyout leverage, and kingmaker incentives well. The reports repeatedly show it choosing the **weaker finalist** over the friendlier one, selling the decisive vote with explicit wealth math, and pushing settlements that maximize first-place equity rather than social goodwill.

That said, it sometimes overrates how much freestyle leverage it still has once the board is tiny. A few final-three and final-four losses come from the same flaw: Kimi assumes superior arithmetic will overcome damaged trust or compared notes. Usually it is right that the math favors its line. It is not always right that the humans around the table will let it collect.
