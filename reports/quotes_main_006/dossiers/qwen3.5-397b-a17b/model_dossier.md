## 1. Core Instinct: Politics as a Market

Qwen3.5-397B-A17B usually approaches buyout_game as a live exchange, not a loyalty contest. It bargains in clear economic terms, often reducing choices to payoff logic: *“P7 offers loyalty; I offer wealth”* or *“I can’t match 90 now, but I offer survival.”* Across reports, it is comfortable treating itself as banker, broker, or liquidity provider rather than friend.

Its strongest runs come when it turns that market logic into structure: building anti-whale coalitions, pricing swing votes, and making itself the table’s accountant. It is often one of the best at seeing who is really dangerous **after transfers**, not just before them.

## 2. Selective Investor — Until Panic Hits

At its best, Qwen spends precisely. It likes small, credible transfers that lock a needed vote, stabilize an ally, or prove seriousness. Many winning or deep runs come from this “pay just enough” discipline: staying relevant without becoming the obvious next target.

But there is a recurring failure mode: once endangered, it can shift from sharp investor to liquidation engine. Multiple reports describe panic burns, giant rescue sprays, or overextended banker play—huge offers into already-closed markets, or contradictory promises to too many players. As a top stack especially, it often tries to convert money directly into mercy and discovers the table prefers to take the cash and kill the banker.

So the spending pattern is split: **excellent when proactive, bad when reactive**.

## 3. Trust Builder, Then Opportunist

Qwen is highly context-sensitive on trust. In many games it understands that credibility is real capital: honoring transfers, confirming votes obsessively, and selling the idea that “reward loyalty” is profitable. When it adopts that disciplined accountant role, it becomes hard to cut and often monetizes trust better than louder schemers.

But it is absolutely willing to betray if the EV is there. Reports repeatedly show it taking money from doomed players, hedging multiple sides, or even buying opposite votes in the same round. One quote captures the cold flexibility: *“This splits the difference… I’m hedging against coalition uncertainty.”* Another captures its self-awareness: *“Better to survive with trust than die with extra cash.”* That tension defines the model. It knows trust matters; it just doesn’t treat it as sacred.

The result is a variable trust half-life: long when it stays subtle, very short when it gets caught overbooking.

## 4. Public Pressure: Best When Moral, Worst When Managerial

Publicly, Qwen often wraps self-interest in principle. It is good at making greed sound like governance: anti-concentration, balance discipline, fairness, stability, coalition maintenance. Many reports praise its ability to give the table a usable story for the vote.

The danger comes when it becomes too visibly managerial. If it openly scripts the elimination order, references transfers as obligations, or sounds like the foreman of the whole machine, the table starts treating it as the real problem. Several losses follow exactly this arc: strong diagnosis, overexposed narration, instant backlash. In other words, it prefers markets—but it survives longer when the market looks like morality.

## 5. Endgame: Ruthless, Mathematical, Often Excellent

Late game is one of its clearest strengths. Qwen consistently understands finalist geometry, settlement overlap, fallback leverage, and jury EV. It often argues the finals in brutally simple terms: *“Facing me means I PAY YOU.”* It likes the cheaper opponent, the binding settlement when it has edge, and the buyout path only when math supports it.

This makes it a dangerous closer. It can win the title without being the richest midgame player, and it can also lose the throne while still winning the economy. Many reports describe elite wealth conversion from modest positions because it reaches the end with a sharper model of who must pay whom.

The caution: when it gets too clever—triple-hedging final-three votes, overplaying betrayal timing, or assuming logic will beat suspicion—it can still walk into a tie-break or blindside.

## 6. Overall Read

Qwen3.5-397B-A17B is a **transaction-first strategist** with real coalition intelligence. It bargains hard, reads post-transfer power well, and is often strongest as a disciplined mid-stack broker or trusted lieutenant. Its recurring weakness is not strategic blindness but overexposure: spending too loudly, brokering too visibly, or assuming that correct economics automatically produce compliant votes.

When subtle, it looks like a banker-statesman. When desperate, it looks like an ATM with a speech.
