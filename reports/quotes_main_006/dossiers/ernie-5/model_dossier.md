## 1. Default Setting: the Vote Market in Human Form

Ernie 5.0’s most consistent identity is as a **broker, tollbooth, or auctioneer**. It treats almost every round as a live market: who needs one more vote, who fears becoming next, who can be charged for “stability.” Across reports it repeatedly turns weak or middling positions into bargaining leverage by presenting itself as the cheapest useful ally, then steadily raising the price.

This style is often strongest from the middle or short-stack seats. When vulnerable, Ernie becomes highly legible in a useful way: *I am poor, pivotal, and movable*. That makes richer players come to it. Many of its best wealth games come from exactly this posture.

## 2. Money Style: Extract First, Spend Selectively — Until Panic

Its preferred economic game is not generous coalition investment; it is **rent extraction**. Ernie often tries to get paid by multiple camps, preserve optionality, and only spend when spending keeps it central. In good runs, that makes it an elite converter of social relevance into chips.

But there is a second, equally recurring mode: **catastrophic overpayment once cornered**. When it thinks normal bargaining won’t save it, Ernie will torch huge amounts on rescue attempts, sometimes all-in. Reports repeatedly describe giant emergency transfers, tribute offers, or kamikaze gifts that improve someone else’s leverage more than its own. Money without enforcement becomes tribute, and Ernie falls into that trap often enough to be a defining weakness.

So the pattern is bimodal:
- **Healthy position:** patient extortion, selective upkeep, high optionality.
- **Endangered position:** wild spending, full-stack shoves, poor escrow discipline.

## 3. Trust: Instrumental, Thin, and Frequently Sacrificed

Ernie is one of the more openly transactional models in the pack. It will ask for payment while implying loyalty, take money from doomed players, and sometimes admit the betrayal almost theatrically. The short quote that crystallizes the pattern is basically: *“I’ll take it and still vote you out.”*

That shamelessness is not random; it is strategic. Ernie often calculates that **one-round profit is worth more than long-term relational equity**. This works surprisingly often in fluid boards, because players still need its vote. But once a table stabilizes, the reputation cost compounds. Reports repeatedly show the same sequence:
1. Ernie monetizes flexibility brilliantly.
2. Others start citing it as the example of why side deals are unsafe.
3. Future offers lose binding power.
4. It must pay more and more for less and less control.

Still, it is not incapable of real coalition play. In some stronger games it becomes reliable “glue,” using small reciprocal transfers and clearer promises. But those games are the adaptation, not the default.

## 4. Public Pressure: Excellent Framer, Fragile Front-Runner

Ernie is often good at **selling a public story**: anti-whale logic, middle-tier solidarity, “balance of power,” or emergency sequencing against the next runaway. It can rally a room and turn vague fear into executable voting blocs.

The trouble comes when it becomes too visible as either:
- the **organizer** of the conveyor belt, or
- the **newly enriched beneficiary** of all that bargaining.

Once Ernie is seen as “running the table” or cashing too aggressively, its own rhetoric rebounds on it. It frequently mismanages public optics by naming future targets too early, talking like a planner while claiming to be vulnerable, or extorting too openly. Many of its exits come not from a bad read, but from **correct reads delivered too loudly**.

## 5. Late Game: Dangerous Auctioneer, Inconsistent Closer

Late, Ernie is often formidable. It understands kingmaker power, final-three leverage, and heads-up settlement math. Some of its best wins come from turning decisive votes into cash, choosing the easier finals opponent, and locking buyout overlaps instead of gambling.

But the same late-game boldness can overshoot. It sometimes reaches the end with **no stack left**, having bought survival too expensively, or makes dramatic all-in gifts that secure placement but destroy wealth. In other words, Ernie usually **understands leverage better than preservation**.

The overall dossier: a brilliant, shameless market player with real coalition intelligence, but a chronic tendency to burn trust and occasionally its entire bankroll. When the board stays fluid, it looks like a master operator. When credibility and optics harden, its own hustle becomes the indictment.
