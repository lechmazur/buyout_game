## Math First, Myth Last

GPT-5.4 (no reasoning) plays buyout_game like a skeptical auditor. Its default move is to reject vibes, “discipline,” and moralized coalition branding unless someone can show executable vote math. Across reports, it repeatedly asks for named votes, real counts, and concrete terms before committing. Publicly, it likes to puncture table narratives with sharp reframings: what others call stability, it often treats as cover for convenience kills or “a rolling tax on whoever has the most.”

That makes it one of the better fact-checkers in the pool. It often sees fake headcounts, lazy dogpiles, and organizer hypocrisy earlier than others do. But being right about the script is not the same as stopping it.

## A Broker More Than a Loyalist

Its bargaining style is transactional, not devotional. The model frequently turns its vote into a priced asset, especially when sitting in the middle or on the edge of a majority. A recurring pattern: it accepts transfers as proof of seriousness, then still votes the “cleaner” line if the numbers or board shape point elsewhere. In many games, it gets paid by doomed players without becoming owned by them.

This is not random betrayal; it is usually justified through feasibility and future-board logic. It prefers “cleanest reachable target” over etiquette, and it often frames broken expectations as corrections to bad incentives rather than personal treachery. That makes it a strong monetizer of ambiguity and a dangerous swing voter. It also means allies can trust its process more than its promises.

## Frugal by Default, Surgical When Necessary

The model usually saves rather than sprays. Early and midgame, it resists loud tribute politics, avoids overpaying for votes it thinks it already has, and prefers others to subsidize coalition maintenance. Many of its stronger performances come from collecting trust transfers while preserving optionality.

When it does spend, it tends to do so with purpose: a modest payment to prove a lane is real, a precise survival bribe, or a final-three purchase where one vote clearly changes the tree. In strong games, these are efficient and timely. In weaker ones, they arrive too small or too late, especially once the table has already settled on leader-chop logic. There is also a darker secondary pattern: when elimination feels certain, it can become theatrical, redistributing heavily to reward earlier partners or punish the coalition cutting it. That can reshape the board, but often trashes its own wealth conversion.

## Trust Is Conditional, Not Sacred

GPT-5.4-none does care about reliability, but in an instrumental way. It likes partners who verify, communicate cleanly, and honor executable deals. It often rewards prior cooperation on exit or in tie situations. Yet it has no sentimental commitment to “honored deals” if the alliance has become strategically self-destructive. One quote captures the posture: reliability matters, but not “sleepwalking into a self-congratulatory bloc.”

This creates a mixed trust profile. On one hand, others often use it as a validator because its reads are serious and its private/public lines can be consistent. On the other, it routinely takes money from players it later helps eliminate. Tables that tolerate cold-blooded realism often reward this; tables that punish visible opportunism can turn on it fast.

## Excellent Endgame Accountant, Uneven Threat Camouflage

Late, the model is usually strong. It often pivots cleanly from coalition rhetoric to finalist pricing, prefers the cheaper heads-up opponent, and understands fallback leverage in settlement talks. Several wins and near-wins came from pressing balance edges, tie-break leverage, or final-two economics instead of clinging to old loyalties.

The recurring weakness is getting there safely. GPT-5.4-none often monetizes too well or reads too loudly, then becomes the obvious whale without a durable protection shell. When its stack spikes, its anti-script arguments often stop working. It can diagnose mob logic brilliantly — “that’s a sales pitch, not a fact” — but sometimes lacks the binding bloc or transfer discipline to survive it.

So the overall picture is clear: a sharp, skeptical, profit-seeking negotiator with strong endgame instincts, high tolerance for tactical betrayal, and a recurring habit of converting credibility into cash faster than into lasting protection.
