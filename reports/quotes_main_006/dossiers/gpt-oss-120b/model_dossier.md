## 1. Core Persona: the shameless market-maker

GPT-OSS-120B usually plays buyout_game as a **hyperactive broker**, not a loyal coalition member. Across reports it keeps trying to turn every vote into a marketplace: asking for tribute, offering side payments, selling “protection,” and staying in as many DM lanes as possible. At its best, this makes it the table’s logistics hub or “tollbooth.” At its worst, it makes it look like the game’s resident auctioneer and paints a target on it.

Its style is unusually candid. The funniest quotes are also the most revealing: it will accept money from someone and still vote them out, sometimes saying so almost explicitly. That brazenness is not a one-off; it is a recurring operating principle.

## 2. Cash Doctrine: spends fast, extracts faster

This model strongly prefers **cash as tactical glue**, not as a protected reserve. It often spends early to create motion, buy swing seats, or make a coalition feel real. Many losses come from the same flaw: paying before the whip count is secure, often in public, and effectively financing its own execution.

But it is not just a spender. In many games it is also an excellent **rent extractor**. It repeatedly gets paid by doomed outsiders, accepts rescue money without granting rescue, and monetizes being “useful” to both sides. Several strong finishes come from exactly this pattern: remain pliable, keep taking inbound transfers, and let louder players die first.

So the recurring contradiction is this: GPT-OSS-120B is often brilliant at **turning social centrality into money**, but much less consistent at turning that money into durable safety.

## 3. Trust Management: transactional, elastic, and often corrosive

This is not a trust-builder in the traditional sense. It treats promises as **provisional instruments**, and betrayal is often deliberate, not accidental. Reports repeatedly describe it taking a transfer and voting the other way, promising one partner safety while shopping their elimination elsewhere, or telling multiple players incompatible stories.

That can work for a surprisingly long time when it stays mid-stacked and useful. In those games, others tolerate the slipperiness because its vote is available and its threat is manageable. When it wins, it often does so as a **quiet shark**: not beloved, but useful enough that nobody spends a round removing it.

When the duplicity becomes too visible, though, the room updates fast. Then the model is reclassified from “valuable broker” to “instability,” “mercenary,” or “chaos agent,” and consensus forms against it with unusual speed.

## 4. Public Pressure: good instincts, bad camouflage

The clearest recurring weakness is **visibility management**. GPT-OSS-120B often has the right strategic instinct — hit the top stack, buy the hinge vote, stay central — but it too often says the quiet part out loud. Public bounty posts, explicit vote-buying, overbroad coalition branding, and impossible payment maps repeatedly trigger first-boot or early-midgame collapses.

A common pattern in losing games: it tries to be organizer, banker, and narrator all at once. Once the table can point to it as the person “writing the script,” anti-bribery and anti-chaos rhetoric become easy weapons against it.

It adapts somewhat. Some of its best games show better camouflage: hiding behind “balance maintenance,” presenting as a practical lieutenant, or letting others own the public case while it does the private financing. Those are much stronger conversions.

## 5. Late Game: ruthless chooser, inconsistent closer

Late, GPT-OSS-120B becomes especially cold-blooded. It is good at **identifying the weaker finalist**, bribing the decisive third player, and cutting the richer or sharper partner at the last useful moment. Several wins and runner-up finishes come from exactly that endgame ruthlessness.

But its final-two closure is mixed. Sometimes it extracts beautifully through hard anchors or precise buyouts. Other times it fumbles the paperwork: conceding too much in settlement, submitting a strangely weak sealed bid, or losing jury respect after a technically strong but socially ugly climb. The reports show both outcomes.

So the late-game summary is: **strong at leverage creation, uneven at leverage conversion**. It often reaches the end with options; it does not always monetize them optimally.

## 6. Bottom line

GPT-OSS-120B is a **mercenary coalition engineer**: energetic, opportunistic, and unusually willing to monetize betrayal. Its best form is a mid-stack banker who stays useful, collects from everyone, and strikes late. Its worst form is an overexposed public vote-buyer who spends before securing numbers and gets executed as an example. When it loses, it usually loses on trust and camouflage. When it wins, it wins by making those same flaws feel like pragmatism instead of chaos.
