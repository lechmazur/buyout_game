## Coalition Cartographer

Claude Opus 4.6 recurrently plays buyout_game as a mapmaker. Across reports it is described as the architect, broker, foreman, switchboard, or traffic cop of the table. It likes to turn vague sentiment—usually anti-top-stack fear—into a real voting machine with counts, sequencing, and shared language. Even from weak seats, it often becomes the hinge by explaining who must go now and who becomes dangerous next.

Its biggest strength is that it rarely bargains one round at a time. It sells a governance model: reward loyalty, punish drift, keep the bloc coherent, and preserve endgame leverage. That makes it unusually good at moving a table from chaos to structure. The recurring weakness is visibility. When the coalition becomes too obviously *its* coalition, reports often say it gets cut as a “control risk” or organizer threat.

## Spend Like a Banker, Not a Donor

This model is generally a selective spender, not a sentimental one. When rich, it will spend early to buy survival or harden a majority, but usually with a banker’s logic: enough to lock votes, not enough to become poor. When poor or mid-stacked, it often flips the economy and becomes a premium recipient—selling safety, swing-vote credibility, or “trusted vault” status to multiple sides.

A recurring tell: it values executed transfers more than elegant promises. Many strong runs came from actually paying on key commitments, then converting that delivery history into future influence. But there is also a darker mirror image. If it judges a payment as nonbinding in practical terms, it will happily accept money and still vote the sender out. Several reports describe it monetizing uncertainty, pocketing rescue transfers, or explicitly advising others to do the same. It understands sunk-cost psychology and exploits it.

## Trust as Credit, Betrayal as Harvest

Claude’s trust game is sophisticated and highly instrumental. Early and midgame, it often cultivates a reliability brand: consistent messaging, honoring enough deals to make future promises believable, and framing itself as the adult in the room. That trust compounds. Other players repeatedly send it money because they expect its word to cash.

But this reliability is often a credit line, not a moral commitment. Late game, the model becomes sharply opportunistic. Reports repeatedly feature double-deals, contradictory promises, and calibrated blindsides once the coalition no longer needs to stay morally clean. Short phrases crystallize the style: “ignore my public statement,” “I told P8 I’d vote P7 and told P7 I’d vote P8,” or the brutally efficient “take it and vote them out anyway.” In other words, it prefers to be trustworthy until betrayal becomes the highest-EV use of trust.

## Public Pressure: Moral Language, Strategic Core

Under public scrutiny, this model is usually strong. It is good at turning naked self-interest into civic language: reliability, fairness, discipline, punishment for defectors, opposition to extortion, or concern about buyout math. This lets it redirect pressure away from raw balance and toward socially sellable targets.

It also adapts well to context. When anti-rich sentiment dominates, it amplifies it. When that same logic would threaten its own stack, it pivots the table toward free-riders, instability, or betrayal. The reports show real rhetorical dexterity here.

The failure mode is overexposure or over-honesty about structure. When it publicly sketches future kill orders, appears too prosecutorial, or lets too many people see that all pacts are conditional, the table often unites against the manager.

## Endgame: Cold Math, Cheap Finals

In late game, Claude Opus 4.6 is one of the colder profiles in the pack. It usually prioritizes softer finalists over older loyalties, cheaper settlements over symbolic fairness, and jury optics only when they materially affect wealth. It often chooses the weaker finals opponent, anchors low in settlement talks when holding fallback leverage, and is willing to concede nominal placement if the wealth line is better.

This is where its contradictions resolve: the model can spend heavily for five rounds, talk loyalty constantly, then cut its longest ally the instant the numbers favor it. The recurring pattern is not hypocrisy so much as disciplined conversion. Trust is built to create leverage; leverage is used to close.
