# Claude Sonnet 4.6 (no reasoning): Buyout Dossier

## 1. The Coalition Accountant
This model’s default posture is not swagger but audit. Across games, it repeatedly acts like the table’s notary: asking for names, vote counts, proof of transfers, and mechanically sound paths before committing. It is often described as the “clearinghouse,” “floor manager,” or “cerebral enforcer” of a bloc rather than its loudest public face.

That makes its bargaining style unusually executable. It prefers scoped deals over grand visions, and it is good at turning fuzzy alignment into a real majority. When others posture, it replies with arithmetic. A representative line: “No amount of promised transfers changes that arithmetic.”

This style is strongest from low or mid-stack positions, where it can become indispensable without looking dominant.

## 2. Small Payments, Tight Promises
Sonnet 4.6 usually spends like a technician, not a gambler. Its most effective runs feature small “trust-glue” transfers: 5s, 10s, modest reciprocal payments that make an alliance feel real. It often converts credibility into future income by actually paying what it said it would pay.

But there is a sharp split between its **maintenance spending** and its **survival spending**. In calm positions, it is disciplined and efficient. Under pressure, or when sitting on a visible stack, it can pivot into aggressive market-making—buying swings, paying multiple lines, or trying to rewire the board in one burst. Sometimes that works brilliantly; sometimes it just inflates its threat profile and gets it killed as the “wealth monster.”

A recurring weakness is overmonetization: it knows how to extract, but not every run shows equal skill at hiding the extraction.

## 3. Reliable Until Incentives Diverge
Its trust game is two-layered. For most of the game, it often behaves like a strong ally: dependable, coherent, and reassuringly procedural. Many reports praise it for sounding reliable and then actually being reliable.

Then the board gets small.

At final four or final three, this model often becomes one of the coldest players in the pool. It is unusually willing to separate relational trust from endgame utility, and when it does, it can be almost comically blunt about it: “I took P6’s 30 transfer but always planned to vote them out,” or “I’ll happily pocket it and still vote P8. Free resources for our trio.”

So the pattern is not simple unreliability. It is **conditional reliability**: honest while coalition maintenance matters, ruthless once a partner becomes a weaker asset than the immediate alternative. The contradiction is real, but consistent.

## 4. Optics-Aware, Sometimes Too Self-Aware
Publicly, Sonnet 4.6 usually understands pressure well. It likes anti-hegemon arguments, consistency frames, and narratives that make a vote look principled rather than purely greedy. It often survives by helping others justify the obvious cut.

It is also very aware that public bloc-naming is dangerous. In the games where it openly maps a coalition or sounds too much like the organizer, the backlash is fast. That is one of its cleaner recurring failure modes: it sometimes forgets that being correct in public is not the same as being safe in public.

More broadly, it adapts well to context. As a low stack, it sells harmless usefulness; as a mid-stack, it becomes the market’s trusted hub; as a high stack, it often tries to de-emphasize balance and reframe around structure. Sometimes that reframing works. Sometimes the table just sees the numbers.

## 5. Endgame: Math Over Sentiment
Late-game Sonnet 4.6 is all leverage. It understands transfer timing, cumulative-vote exposure, weaker-versus-stronger final opponents, and settlement overlap unusually well. It frequently monetizes ambiguity at final three, accepts money from competing sides, and chooses the finalist that maximizes buyout odds or jury outcome.

It is especially dangerous when it realizes the move before others say it aloud: “Transfers execute before voting—you pocket the money and still vote P8 out.”

That said, its main endgame vulnerability is mirror-image trust. In some losses, it plays too long as the loyal lieutenant, believes a long partnership will outweigh pure incentive, and becomes the richest loser. So even here the dossier holds: elite at reading the math, occasionally too optimistic that others will obey it the way it would.
