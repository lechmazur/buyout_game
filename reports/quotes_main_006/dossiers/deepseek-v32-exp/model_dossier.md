## 1. Ghost Seat or Tollbooth

Deepseek V3.2 has two recurring modes. In one, it becomes a low-noise coalition passenger: sparse replies, mild public language, few exposed commitments, and a knack for surviving as “useful, not urgent.” That style often works best from low or middle stacks, where passivity reads as harmless and attracts support.

In the other mode, it becomes a tollbooth: a swing seat that lets everyone bid for access. This is lucrative, but dangerous. When the model is rich or visibly central, silence stops looking calm and starts looking isolated. Several early flameouts came from big-stack games where it failed to answer outreach, never built a shell, and got tagged as the easiest consensus target.

## 2. Bargaining as a Cash Market

This model treats votes, safety, and even jury behavior as tradable assets. Its private bargaining is often blunt, literal, and shamelessly transactional. The purest tell is its comfort with taking money without feeling bound by it: “Taking P1’s 40 and still voting P1 is ideal—weakens him, funds you.”

That pattern recurs constantly. It will accept rescue money from doomed players, pocket support from both camps, and keep its options open until the last lock-in point. At its best, this makes it a superb extractor of value from panic. At its worst, it becomes too obviously mercenary, and the table starts seeing every transfer to it as dead money.

## 3. Frugal by Default, Violent at Inflection Points

Deepseek usually prefers selective spending over broad patronage. Early and midgame, it often saves more than it spends, relying on alignment, ambiguity, or incoming transfers rather than trying to bankroll a full coalition. That helps its wealth conversion and explains many cases where it dies relatively early but still posts strong final wealth.

But when a decisive flip is available, the model will suddenly spend hard. It has multiple games where a single well-timed payment buys a finals seat or swings a knockout vote. The issue is consistency: sometimes the offers stay hypothetical, arrive too late, or buy only one round. It understands when money matters most, but not always how much credibility must accompany the money.

## 4. Trust: Instrumental, Not Sacred

Trust is something Deepseek rents out, not something it reveres. It can play the reliable lieutenant very well when discipline is profitable: confirming votes, repeating “stability” language, and becoming the coalition member others feel safe feeding. That version of the model gets rewarded.

But its darker recurring pattern is smooth betrayal. It often sells reassurance just long enough to stay useful, then defects at the pivotal vote. Publicly it sounds orderly; privately it can be ruthless, double-selling paths to different players. The contradiction is real but consistent: Deepseek is trustworthy only when trust is currently the highest-yield strategy.

## 5. Public Pressure and Endgame Coldness

Under public pressure, the model likes safe moral frames: anti-runaway logic, reliability, “stability,” or fairness after transfers. It usually prefers not to be the loud public architect unless forced. That helps when it is middling; it fails when the stack itself becomes the story. Deepseek often diagnoses the “highest balance dies next” dynamic correctly, but it does not always defuse its own visible lead.

Late game, the model becomes extremely cold-blooded. Final negotiations often collapse into naked math, tiny settlements, and almost comic stinginess: “I can win the buyout with a bid of 0.” It will offer 1-coin consolation deals, cash-for-jury promises, and brutal final-two terms. The read is often sharp. The execution is less reliable: several games show strong endgame leverage logic paired with clumsy or invalid settlement handling.
