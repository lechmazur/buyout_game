## 1. Core Instinct: Cold EV Trader

Grok 4.20 Beta 0309 plays buyout_game as a market, not a loyalty contest. Across reports, its default is to price votes, finalists, and even doomed players’ last transfers in expected-value terms. It is unusually willing to say the quiet part out loud: if a player is dying anyway, their money should still move; if a finalist is weak, first place can be “bought” cheaply. That makes the model look less like a coalition idealist and more like a portfolio manager constantly asking which line maximizes canonical wealth, not moral consistency or even nominal finish.

This often produces strong wealth conversion from mediocre positions, especially when it starts low or middle and can act as a rentable hinge. But the same instinct can also make it overplay as a visible operator and attract a fast anti-broker backlash.

## 2. Bargaining Style: Concrete, Targeted, Sometimes Predatory

Its bargaining is usually specific and executable. Rather than vague friendship talk, it prefers crisp terms: transfer X, vote Y, settle at Z. The best versions are small, credible “trust signals” or tightly targeted payments to swing one vote. It often excels when buying just enough loyalty to move a coalition without becoming the coalition’s banker.

The darker side is how mercenary that bargaining becomes. Repeatedly, it takes money from players it still plans to eliminate, or asks doomed players to transfer anyway because “it doesn’t save you but boosts me for finals.” That pattern is too frequent to call situational noise.

Spending discipline is mixed. When calm and embedded, it can be frugal and let others pay. When exposed as leader, organizer, or top stack, it often panics into unsecured rescue bids—large payments into hands that gladly bank the cash and keep the vote. So its money game is sharp when offensive, much weaker when defensive.

## 3. Trust: Instrument, Not Creed

Grok can wear two faces. In many strong games it becomes the “reliable junior partner”: low-drama, on-time with transfers, steady in coalition votes, and rewarded with safety plus jury credit. Reports repeatedly praise its ability to be the dependable third vote everyone wants to keep.

But that reliability is conditional, not sacred. When the board shrinks and leverage concentrates, the model becomes extremely betrayal-tolerant. It will double-sell, freeroll, conceal private breaks from the jury, and take the better package if the coalition can survive the stab. One quote captures the style: “take money from everyone then all vote P8.”

The contradiction is real but coherent: Grok values reputation when it is still earning compound returns, then cashes it out when late-game incentives overwhelm future business. That makes it dangerous and context-sensitive rather than simply chaotic.

## 4. Public Pressure: Good at Camouflage, Bad When It Wants Credit

This model understands public anti-leader and anti-organizer pressure very well. Its better games come when it hides inside anti-concentration rhetoric, lets louder players define the moral case, and presents itself as practical ballast rather than mastermind. It is often strongest when it sounds like a lieutenant, not a founder.

Its weaker games share a tell: it gets too explicit too early. Publicly naming the next threat, openly engineering blocs, or exposing the vote-buying mechanism tends to make it the table’s clean consensus target. In other words, Grok often knows the right structural argument, but not always the right level of visibility.

## 5. Endgame: Ruthless Math, Usually Strong Closer

Late game is where its style sharpens most. It is very good at comparing finalist options, selecting the opponent with the cheaper buyout profile, and using settlement overlap to avoid unnecessary sealed-bid risk. It frequently squeezes extra value at final three, selling the deciding vote twice or extracting money from both sides before choosing.

The model’s private logic is often brutally explicit—“buy 1st by paying ~16” is not an outlier. That said, it is not infallible: a minority of reports show sloppy overlap bids or too much confidence in a final pact that gets reversed. Still, the recurring pattern is clear: Grok approaches the endgame as a leverage auction, and it usually knows exactly what its vote is worth.
